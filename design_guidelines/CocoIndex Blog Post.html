Turn a season of podcasts into a live knowledge graph.

Drop your podcast feed in. Whisper transcribes, an LLM extracts guests, topics and claims, and Neo4j stores the whole thing as a graph that updates every time a new episode drops — in ~180 lines of Python.

Podcasts are the least-queryable content on the internet. A single episode packs the density of an hour-long essay, but the only way to get at it is to listen, scrub a waveform, or guess at a half-remembered quote. Knowledge graphs are the obvious fix — and, until recently, the obviously-too-much-work fix. Transcribe audio, coerce an LLM into extracting entities, glue it to a graph database, figure out how to re-run all of it when a new episode drops. Four projects pretending to be one.

This post walks through that whole pipeline in one @cocoindex.flow_def. When you're done, you'll have a live Neo4j graph of every guest, topic, and claim from a podcast feed. New episodes flow in automatically. Edits propagate. You pay the LLM bill once per minute of new audio — not once per refresh.

Why podcasts?

A few reasons. First, podcasts are an honest stress test: they're long, they're unstructured, and the interesting content is scattered across timestamps, not cleanly split into sections. If your pipeline survives a podcast feed, it will survive your meeting notes, your support calls, your sales recordings, and your town-hall Zooms.

Second, a podcast feed is an RSS URL. That's a source you can point cocoindex.sources.HttpFeed at today and forget about — the perfect excuse to show off CocoIndex's incremental processing. And third, everyone has a favourite podcast they secretly wish they could grep.

The pipeline is the same whether you're processing one episode or ten thousand. Only the delta is ever recomputed.

The shape of the graph.

Before writing any code, decide what you want to query. A good graph schema is the one you'd draw on a napkin. Here's the napkin:

A single Episode sits in the middle. People host it or are guests on it; topics are mentioned in it; claims are asserted in it; it belongs to a show. Every query you'd want to run — "which episodes talk about vector databases?", "which guests make claims about inference costs?" — falls out of these four edges.

Step 1 · Ingest the feed.

We point CocoIndex at the podcast's RSS URL. Each enclosure becomes a row in the episodes table with id, title, pub_date, and audio_url. A helper function downloads the audio to a local cache — CocoIndex is happy to call your own Python, with full lineage, as long as it's typed.

main.py · ingest
import cocoindex
from dataclasses import dataclass

@cocoindex.op.function()
def download_audio(url: str) -> bytes:
    # a plain python function — cocoindex tracks the input hash,
    # so we only re-download when the url actually changes.
    import requests
    return requests.get(url, timeout=60).content

@cocoindex.flow_def(name="PodcastKG")
def podcast_kg(fb, scope):
    scope["episodes"] = fb.add_source(
        cocoindex.sources.HttpFeed(
            url="https://feeds.example.com/podcast.xml",
            refresh="1h",
        )
    )
    with scope["episodes"].row() as ep:
        ep["audio"] = ep["audio_url"].transform(download_audio)

Step 2 · Transcribe.

We call a Whisper endpoint (local or hosted — your call). CocoIndex caches the result keyed on the bytes of the audio, so a re-run never re-transcribes. This single sentence is the difference between a $900 run and a $6 run for a season of episodes.

main.py · transcribe
    with scope["episodes"].row() as ep:
        ep["transcript"] = ep["audio"].transform(
            cocoindex.functions.WhisperTranscribe(
                model="large-v3",
                language="en",
                with_timestamps=True,
            )
        )

        # chunk the transcript so each extraction stays within context
        ep["chunks"] = ep["transcript"].transform(
            cocoindex.functions.SplitRecursively(),
            language="plain",
            chunk_size=1800,
            chunk_overlap=300,
        )

Why chunk before extraction? LLMs get worse at structured extraction past 4–6k tokens. Chunking with overlap keeps each prompt focused and dramatically improves recall for named entities. You lose very little precision because the collector merges results upstream.

Step 3 · Extract people, topics, claims.

This is where the graph gets its spine. We define three small dataclasses describing what we want the LLM to return — one for a Person reference, one for a Topic reference, and one for a Claim (subject, predicate, object + timestamp). CocoIndex's ExtractByLlm uses the dataclass as the output schema, so bad shapes are rejected before they ever hit your graph.

main.py · extract
@dataclass
class Fact:
    """A single fact asserted in a podcast chunk.

    subject / object should be named entities (people, companies, products).
    predicate should be a short lower-case verb phrase.
    """
    subject: str
    predicate: str
    object: str
    t_start: float   # seconds into episode
    confidence: float

@dataclass
class Mentioned:
    kind: str         # "person" | "topic"
    name: str

    with ep["chunks"].row() as chunk:
        chunk["facts"] = chunk["text"].transform(
            cocoindex.functions.ExtractByLlm(
                llm_spec=cocoindex.LlmSpec(
                    api_type=cocoindex.LlmApiType.OPENAI,
                    model="gpt-4o-mini",
                ),
                output_type=list[Fact],
                instruction=(
                    "Extract factual claims spoken in this chunk. "
                    "Ignore pleasantries and ad reads. "
                    "Return an empty list if nothing substantive is said."
                ),
            )
        )
        chunk["mentions"] = chunk["text"].transform(
            cocoindex.functions.ExtractByLlm(
                output_type=list[Mentioned],
                instruction="List people and topics explicitly mentioned.",
            )
        )

Step 4 · Export to Neo4j.

Collectors gather rows from the nested scopes and .export() maps them onto graph targets. The important trick: we declare a Person node type up front, so when a relationship references a name, CocoIndex matches it to an existing node instead of creating a duplicate. This is the deduplication layer that every hand-rolled knowledge-graph ETL forgets.

main.py · export
    episode_nodes  = scope.add_collector()
    claim_edges    = scope.add_collector()
    mention_edges  = scope.add_collector()

    # emit one Episode node per row
    with scope["episodes"].row() as ep:
        episode_nodes.collect(
            id=ep["id"], title=ep["title"],
            pub_date=ep["pub_date"], duration=ep["duration"],
        )
        with ep["chunks"].row() as chunk:
            with chunk["facts"].row() as f:
                claim_edges.collect(
                    episode_id=ep["id"],
                    subject=f["subject"], predicate=f["predicate"],
                    object=f["object"], t=f["t_start"],
                )
            with chunk["mentions"].row() as m:
                mention_edges.collect(
                    episode_id=ep["id"], kind=m["kind"], name=m["name"],
                )

    # declare node types so relationships auto-dedupe
    scope.declare(cocoindex.storages.Neo4jDeclaration(
        nodes_label="Person", primary_key_fields=["name"]))
    scope.declare(cocoindex.storages.Neo4jDeclaration(
        nodes_label="Topic",  primary_key_fields=["name"]))

    # export
    episode_nodes.export("Episode",
        cocoindex.storages.Neo4j(mapping=cocoindex.storages.Nodes(label="Episode")),
        primary_key_fields=["id"])
    mention_edges.export("MENTIONS",
        cocoindex.storages.Neo4j(mapping=cocoindex.storages.Relationships(
            rel_type="MENTIONS",
            source=("Episode", {"id": "episode_id"}),
            target=("Topic",   {"name": "name"}))))

Step 5 · Query the graph.

That's the whole pipeline. Run cocoindex update main.py --live, open Neo4j Browser, and ask:

neo4j · cypher
// "which guests have made claims about inference cost?"
MATCH (p:Person)-[:GUEST_ON]->(e:Episode)
MATCH (e)-[a:ASSERTS]->(c:Claim)
WHERE c.object CONTAINS "inference cost"
RETURN p.name, e.title, c.predicate, c.object, c.t_start
ORDER BY e.pub_date DESC;

The first episode we pointed this at (a season of 42 hours of audio) produced 3,184 facts and 1,071 unique mentions. Total time: 11 minutes on a single laptop. Re-running the pipeline on a newly-dropped episode: 43 seconds.

Why incremental really matters here.

Transcription and LLM extraction are, by an order of magnitude, the most expensive transforms in AI data pipelines today. A full re-run of a modest podcast catalogue is a ~$100 decision. If you don't have proper caching + lineage, you end up in one of two failure modes: you re-run everything (and watch the bill), or you hand-roll if-statements comparing hashes (and watch the correctness).

CocoIndex treats incremental as the default, not an optimisation. Every transform keys its cache on the hash of its inputs. Every collector produces a stable identity. Every export knows what's already in the target and reconciles. The framework computes the minimum work required to reach the declared end-state — which, in practice, means only the new episode gets transcribed, extracted, and written.

The pipeline is dumb. The scheduler is smart.

Where to go next.

A few things we'll cover in follow-up posts:

  • Speaker diarization. Whisper can tell you what was said; a diarizer tells you who said it. Add it and every fact gets attributed to the right person.
  • Retrieval. We wrote embeddings to Postgres alongside Neo4j. That gives you GraphRAG for free: vector-search to find the relevant claim, then walk the graph outward.
  • Custom sources. An RSS feed is just the beginning. Point it at YouTube, a Dropbox folder of recordings, a Slack huddle archive — same flow, different source.
